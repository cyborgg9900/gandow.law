How to carry out due diligence on a business

By Milan Schuster

In today’s fast-paced business world, it’s crucial to perform due diligence on a business to have a comprehensive understanding of a company before engaging in any transaction.

Due diligence is the key to this understanding, providing insights into a company’s financial, legal, and operational aspects. Here, we’ll take you on a journey through the due diligence process, exploring its importance, various types, and how to prepare for and conduct a thorough review. By the end, you’ll be equipped with the knowledge and tools to make informed decisions and minimise potential risks in your business transactions.

Short Summary

  • Due diligence is a comprehensive assessment of a company’s financial records, business transactions and legal compliance. It is the ultimate business risk assessment.
  • It usually involves four components – financial, legal, commercial and reputational, to assess potential for success.
  • Preparation includes assembling an experienced team with clear objectives on a timeline to conduct a thorough review of the target company.

Understanding the Due Diligence Process

The due diligence process enables investors, businesses, and individuals to assess the target company’s value, potential risks, and liabilities before entering into a transaction or investment.

Key Components of Due Diligence

A comprehensive due diligence process comprises four key components: financial, legal, commercial, and reputational due diligence. Each of these components plays a vital role in evaluating the target company’s overall health and potential for success.

For instance, examining a company’s industry and competition during due diligence can help assess profit margins and identify which companies have a competitive advantage. Similarly, analysing a target company’s business plan and model can provide insights into its viability and integration capability.

Conducting due diligence on multiple companies within a given industry can also offer investors a comprehensive understanding of the industry’s current state and which firms are leading the sector.

The Role of Due Diligence in Risk Management

Due diligence is the cornerstone of savvy business practices, serving as a crucial tool for identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks associated with business transactions. This process involves a meticulous examination of a business entity or an individual’s economic, legal, fiscal, and financial circumstances. The primary objective of this exercise is to unearth potential hazards and liabilities that could potentially surface during a transaction, thereby enabling businesses to make informed decisions.

There are various forms of due diligence, each targeting a specific aspect of risk management. Financial due diligence, for instance, delves into the financial health and stability of a company. It scrutinizes financial statements, assesses profitability trends, and evaluates overall financial performance to provide a clear picture of the company’s fiscal well-being.

Legal due diligence, on the other hand, focuses on the company’s adherence to laws and regulations. It encompasses a thorough review of legal documents, contracts, litigation history, and regulatory compliance, ensuring that the company has been operating within the bounds of the law.

Commercial due diligence examines the company’s market position, competitive landscape, customer base, and growth prospects, while reputational due diligence investigates public perception, brand image, and customer satisfaction levels.

By shedding light on significant risks and providing an avenue to address them preemptively, due diligence serves as a protective shield for businesses. It empowers them with the knowledge they need to safeguard their interests, make strategic decisions, and navigate the complexities of business transactions with confidence.

Types of Due Diligence: A Closer Look

Hard due diligence focuses on the financial and legal aspects of a company, while soft due diligence assesses the human components, such as culture and management.

In the following sections, we’ll examine the four main types of due diligence in greater detail: financial, legal, commercial, and reputational.

Financial Due Diligence

  1. Historical Financial Analysis: This involves evaluating historical trends in financial statements, such as revenue growth, margin analysis, and working capital trends, with a focus on the specifics of the Irish market and economy.
  2. Quality of Earnings (QoE): Analysis of the company’s adjusted EBITDA by identifying and quantifying non-recurring items, one-off revenues or expenses, and other non-operational impacts that are specific to the Irish business environment.
  3. Working Capital Analysis: Understanding the nature and timing of working capital items is crucial for structuring the purchase price in a transaction, taking into account Ireland’s specific financial regulations and standards.
  4. Contingent Liabilities: Identification of potential liabilities, such as pending or potential lawsuits, unresolved disputes, or underfunded pension obligations, according to Irish law.
  5. Forecast and Projections Review: Evaluation of the feasibility and underlying assumptions of the company’s financial projections, considering Ireland’s economic outlook.
  6. Capital Expenditure Requirements: Understanding the capital investments required to sustain or grow the business within the context of Ireland’s economic infrastructure.
  7. Customer and Supplier Concentration: Evaluating the concentration of revenue among top customers and the reliance on key suppliers, especially those based in or serving the Irish market.
  8. Debt and Financing Analysis: Detailed analysis of the company’s debt schedule, interest rates, covenants, and other financial instruments, according to Irish financial regulations.
  9. Off-Balance Sheet Items: Identifying items not readily apparent on the balance sheet, such as leases or other off-balance sheet financing, according to Irish accounting standards.
  10. Tax Compliance and Positions: Reviewing tax returns and evaluating the company’s tax strategy, any outstanding tax liabilities, or potential tax savings, considering Irish tax laws and regulations.
  11. Integration and Synergy Analysis: For strategic buyers, understanding where financial synergies may exist post-acquisition, especially when integrating businesses within the Irish market.
  12. Foreign Currency and Hedging: If the company operates in multiple countries, it’s crucial to understand foreign currency exposures and any hedging strategies in place, especially those involving the Euro.
  13. Internal Control Assessment: Evaluating the robustness of the company’s internal controls over financial reporting, according to Irish corporate governance standards.
  14. Industry Benchmarking: Comparing the company’s financial performance and metrics against industry peers, with a focus on Irish market standards.
  15. Inventory Evaluation: Assessing the quality, age, and salebility of inventory, considering the specificities of the Irish market.
  16. Employee and Executive Compensation: Understanding the company’s obligations and commitments in terms of compensation, bonuses, and retirement benefits, according to Irish labour laws and regulations.
  17. Contract Review: Understanding the financial implications of key contracts, including long-term agreements, exclusivity clauses, and termination provisions, under Irish contract law.
  18. Intellectual Property Valuation: For companies where IP is a significant asset, a valuation might be necessary, according to Irish IP law.

Legal Due Diligence

Legal due diligence is an essential part of buying a business. It involves a thorough examination of all legal aspects of the business to identify potential risks, liabilities, and legal obligations that could affect the transaction’s value. Here is what it typically involves:

  1. Corporate Records Review: This involves examining the business’s incorporation documents, byelaws, minutes of board meetings, and records of share issues and transfers. The purpose is to ensure the company has been properly formed and maintained, and to understand the company’s ownership structure.
  2. Contracts Examination: All contracts the business is party to should be reviewed. This includes customer contracts, supplier agreements, leases, licensing agreements, and employment contracts. You’ll want to understand the obligations, terms, and any potential liabilities in these contracts.
  3. Legal Compliance: This involves checking that the business complies with relevant laws and regulations. For instance, you would check if the company is in compliance with employment laws, environmental regulations, data protection laws, tax laws, etc.
  4. Litigation and Disputes: You need to know if the business is involved in any ongoing or potential litigation, disputes, or investigations. This could significantly impact the value and operation of the business.
  5. Intellectual Property: If the business owns intellectual property (IP), such as patents, copyrights, trademarks, or trade secrets, these should be thoroughly examined. You’ll want to confirm the business owns these IPs, that they are valid, and if there are any disputes or infringements.
  6. Employment and Labour Law: This involves reviewing employee contracts, benefits plans, labour union agreements, and any pending or potential labour disputes.
  7. Real Estate: If the business owns real estate, you’ll want to review the title deeds, zoning compliance, leases, and any liens or encumbrances on the property.
  8. Environmental Issues: If the business is in an industry where environmental issues are pertinent, you’ll need to check for compliance with environmental laws and the potential for liabilities from past or ongoing environmental issues.

Commercial Due Diligence

Commercial due diligence involves evaluating a target business’s market position, competitive landscape, and overall commercial viability. This type of due diligence helps assess the target company’s business model, its operational processes, and its technology infrastructure.

In addition, commercial due diligence includes analysing the target company’s intangible assets, such as goodwill, brand recognition, and intellectual property assets like trademarks and patents. Assessing a company’s commercial viability can offer potential investors valuable insights into the target company’s strengths, weaknesses, and overall potential for success.

Reputational Due Diligence

Reputational due diligence is the process of investigating a company’s reputation, including potential issues and red flags. Various methods, such as media searches, social media monitoring, and interviews with past and current employees, are employed to gather information about the target company’s reputation.

By conducting a thorough reputational due diligence, potential investors can uncover any hidden issues or liabilities that may not be apparent from financial statements and legal documents alone.

Setting a Realistic Timeline

Determining an appropriate timeframe for completing the due diligence process is crucial in order to plan and manage expectations.

A realistic timeline enables efficient resource allocation, allows for the identification of potential risks and challenges, and ensures that goals and objectives are achieved within the prescribed timeframe.

To establish a realistic timeline, consider the complexity of the process, the resources available, and the timeline for each step of the process. Furthermore, factor in any potential risks or challenges that may arise during the process, and adjust the timeline accordingly to account for unforeseen circumstances.

Establishing Clear Objectives

Clear objectives help prioritise work, eliminate ambiguity, and offer a sense of purpose and direction.

Some techniques for establishing clear objectives include setting measurable goals, dividing objectives into smaller tasks, and assigning deadlines. By regularly monitoring progress and measuring success, you can ensure that your due diligence process remains on track and achieves the desired outcomes.

Assessing Operational Efficiency

In addition to reviewing financial statements and legal documents, assessing operational efficiency is a crucial part of the due diligence process. This involves evaluating a company’s operations, processes, and potential areas of risk to gain a comprehensive understanding of its overall performance and stability.

To effectively assess operational efficiency, analyze the target company’s organizational structure, its operational processes, and its technology infrastructure. Identifying potential risks and challenges in these areas can help you pinpoint areas for improvement and ensure a smooth integration process following the acquisition or merger.

Utilising a Due Diligence Checklist

A well-structured checklist helps you keep track of the information you’ve gathered and the tasks you’ve completed, allowing you to focus on the most critical aspects of the due diligence process.

Every business acquisition is different and you should speak to a commercial legal expert to help you create a checklist for your circumstances. Here is an example:

  1. Corporate Records:
  • Review incorporation documents
  • Examine byelaws and amendments
  • Check minutes of board meetings
  1. Financial Due Diligence:
  • Review financial statements
  • Analyse historical financial trends
  • Evaluate quality of earnings
  1. Legal Due Diligence:
  • Examine contracts and agreements
  • Assess legal compliance
  • Investigate ongoing or potential litigation
  1. Operational Due Diligence:
  • Evaluate operational processes
  • Assess efficiency of management team
  • Review customer and supplier relationships
  1. Intellectual Property:
  • Validate ownership of IPs (patents, trademarks, copyrights)
  • Assess any IP disputes
  1. Employee and Labour Law:
  • Review employee contracts and benefits
  • Check compliance with labour regulations
  1. Real Estate and Assets:
  • Review property deeds and leases
  • Check condition and value of physical assets
  1. Environmental Issues:
  • Evaluate compliance with environmental regulations
  • Assess potential liabilities from environmental issues
  1. Tax Compliance:
  • Review tax returns and payments
  • Evaluate tax planning strategies
  1. Market Analysis:
  • Understand the competitive landscape
  • Evaluate market trends and growth prospects

Remember, this is a general checklist. Depending on the specific business and industry, some additional checks may be necessary. Always consider engaging professionals (like lawyers, accountants, etc.) to ensure a thorough due diligence process.

Post-Due Diligence Actions

Post-due diligence actions help you make informed decisions, address identified risks, and renegotiate terms if necessary.

The significance of post-due diligence actions lies in their ability to provide an extensive evaluation of the target company once the primary due diligence process has been completed.

By executing post-due diligence actions, buyers can uncover any potential risks or matters that may have been missed during the initial evaluation, enabling them to make more educated decisions and reduce any potential risks prior to finalising the transaction.

What is an example of due diligence in business?

Examples of due diligence include investigating the financials of a company before investing, researching a person’s background before hiring them, and reviewing environmental impact reports before committing to a construction project.

Due diligence is an important part of any decision-making process. It helps to ensure that all relevant information is taken into account and that any risks are minimized. By taking the time to do due diligence, organizations can make better decisions and avoid costly mistakes.

Why is due diligence important?

Due diligence is important as it allows for informed decision-making and reduces potential risks before investing or entering into a transaction. It thus safeguards against financial losses and ensures long-term success.

What types of due diligence are there?

Due diligence typically involves financial, legal, commercial, and reputational assessments.

How can I prepare for a due diligence process?

Prepare for the due diligence process by assembling a team, setting a timeline, and establishing objectives.
